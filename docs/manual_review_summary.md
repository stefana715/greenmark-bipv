# GBRS Manual Review Summary: PV and BIPV Treatment

**Review date:** April 2026  
**Reviewer:** Systematic extraction from official certification manuals  
**Scope:** 21 Green Building Rating Systems assessed for photovoltaic (PV) and building-integrated photovoltaic (BIPV) treatment across credit language, application coverage, feasibility study requirements, ambition levels, verification, and innovation pathways.

**Data quality codes used throughout:**
- `[VERIFIED]` — data extracted directly from the official certification manual
- `[PARTIAL]` — data extracted from a related document (addendum, taxonomy report) but full manual unavailable
- `[EXISTING]` — data retained from scoring matrix baseline; no manual file was available or the mapped file turned out to be a different system

---

## 1. LEED v4.1 BD+C (USA) [VERIFIED]

LEED treats PV under two credits: EAc-RE (Renewable Energy, 5/110 pts) and IPc1 (Integrative Process, 1/110 pts). EAc-RE explicitly names "a photovoltaic array located on the project site" as a qualifying system, using a greenhouse-gas-based metric rather than a percentage-of-energy approach, which allows high-efficiency buildings to achieve points with modest PV installations. There is no distinction between BIPV and building-attached PV (BAPV) anywhere in the credit language; the system treats all on-site solar generation equivalently regardless of how PV is mounted or integrated into the building fabric. IPc1 encourages solar analysis as part of early design discovery but does not mandate a solar feasibility study, and no explicit pathway exists for facade or window PV applications beyond their contribution to the energy model.

The overall BIPV environment in LEED is permissive but passive: BIPV is neither rewarded nor discouraged, and its value is entirely indirect through energy or GHG savings. The Exemplary Performance threshold (100% on-site RE via Innovation credit) creates a theoretical incentive for maximising generation area through facade integration, but this pathway is rarely pursued in practice. Verification is limited to commissioning; no ongoing PV performance monitoring is required.

## 2. BREEAM NC 2018 (UK) [VERIFIED]

BREEAM addresses renewables through two interacting credits: Ene01 (carbon reduction, up to 9 standard + 5 exemplary credits, ~11% of weighted score) and Ene04 (low carbon design, up to 3 credits). The critical finding is that Ene04 Criterion 9 is **mandatory** — a Low/Zero Carbon (LZC) technology feasibility study must be completed at Concept Design stage, covering all LZC technologies including PV. This is the strongest mandatory design-integration requirement among the points-based systems reviewed. However, the word "BIPV" never appears in the scheme; all renewables are assessed under the generic LZC technology category defined by EU Directive 2009/28/EC.

BREEAM's ambition level is high for a voluntary scheme: Ene01 Criteria 8-9 require net-zero and net-negative carbon for exemplary credits. Facade PV can contribute to the LZC feasibility study and the whole-building carbon model, but there is no dedicated credit pathway distinguishing building-integrated from building-attached configurations. Novel LZC technologies (which could include BIPV facades) require SBEM-Q approval for the model to accept their output, creating a modest barrier to crediting innovative BIPV applications.

## 3. Green Mark GM:2021 (Singapore) [VERIFIED]

Green Mark offers the strongest direct PV incentive among the systems reviewed: up to 20 bonus points (20% of the 100-point base) for renewable energy, plus certification tiers where Zero Energy (ZE) buildings must generate 100% of energy from RE and Positive Energy (PE) buildings must generate 115% from on-site RE only. The Stage 1 and Stage 2 Verification process makes solar feasibility effectively mandatory — technical drawings and expected generation calculations must be submitted at both design and post-occupancy stages. A 12-month post-occupancy energy audit verifies actual PV performance, making Green Mark one of only three systems (alongside LBC and MINERGIE) with genuine operational verification.

BIPV is not named as an explicit credit term in the base GM:2021 scheme, but the Super Low Energy (SLE) programme explicitly recognises facade BIPV as a contributing technology and the PE tier requirement (115% from on-site generation only) effectively incentivises building-integrated approaches to maximise generation surface area. Green Mark is unique in Asia for the breadth of its renewable energy integration pathway and the rigour of its operational verification requirements.

## 4. Green Star v1.3 Buildings (Australia) [EXISTING]

No Green Star manual was available for direct review. The file mapped to Green Star (6.03_building_criteria_en.pdf) was found to contain Passive House criteria, not Green Star content. Data is retained from the scoring matrix baseline. Green Star allocates approximately 25 points for GHG reduction in its Energy category, with PV contributing via whole-building carbon modelling. The 6-Star "World Leader" tier effectively requires net-zero carbon, making PV essential for high-achieving projects. An Innovation Challenge pathway can reward novel BIPV integration, but no dedicated BIPV credit exists and no mandatory solar feasibility study is required.

## 5. ASGB / China GB/T 50378-2019 (China) [VERIFIED]

Section 5.2.16 of the standard governs renewable energy utilization (节能与能源利用 — Energy Saving and Energy Utilization), awarding up to 10/400 points (~2.5%). The standard assesses three RE categories: domestic hot water, cooling/heating load, and electricity — with PV counting toward the electricity RE ratio (Re). The Chinese-language verbatim text defines renewable energy as 风能、太阳能、水能、生物质能、地热能和海洋能 (wind, solar, water, biomass, geothermal, ocean) without any distinction for BIPV. Operational assessment (运行评价) requires verified performance data, making this one of the few systems with formal operational verification requirements, though the credit weight is the lowest of any system reviewed.

There is no mandatory solar feasibility study, no facade or window PV pathway, and no BIPV language anywhere in the reviewed sections. The standard's primary focus on minimum renewable contribution thresholds (1% electricity from RE for credits) positions it as a compliance floor rather than an innovation driver.

## 6. BEAM Plus v2.0 NB (Hong Kong) [VERIFIED]

BEAM Plus is the **only system in the dataset that explicitly names BIPV in its credit language**. EU5a (Solar Energy Feasibility Study, 1 credit) requires: "Conduct a feasibility study to evaluate the potential of standalone and building-integrated installation in harnessing solar energy including photovoltaic and solar water heating. 1. Consideration of PV, BIPV or solar thermal potential installation." This constitutes the clearest recognition of BIPV as a distinct technology pathway among all 21 systems reviewed. EU5b (On-site Renewable Energy Application, up to 10 credits) then awards credits based on percentage of annual building energy from renewables, with solar PV explicitly listed.

The limitation is ambition level: EU5b's maximum threshold is only 2% of annual energy from renewables for full credit — extremely modest. The feasibility study (EU5a) earns 1 credit but does not mandate implementation. BEAM Plus's explicit BIPV recognition in EU5a is therefore a notable procedural strength that is not matched by ambitious performance targets. The feasibility study must be endorsed by a Locally Qualified Professional with at least 3 years of RE experience, adding procedural rigour.

## 7. CASBEE BD 2024 (Japan) [VERIFIED]

CASBEE uses a ratio-based Building Environmental Efficiency (BEE = Q/LR) scoring system rather than points, making direct comparison with other systems difficult. Renewable energy is assessed under LR1.4.3 (Expanding Adoption of Non-Fossil Energy), where PV is described as a "converted use of natural energy" in sub-item LR1.3. The 2024 version explicitly cites SDG 7.2.1 (affordable clean energy) in reference to solar power, reflecting alignment with international sustainability frameworks. However, BIPV is not mentioned anywhere, no mandatory solar study is required, and the LR1.4.3 sub-item is an optional enhancement rather than a baseline requirement.

CASBEE-BD 2024 introduces ZEB (Zero Energy Building) pathway references and requires post-occupancy data submission through the DECC database (pages 256-259), which represents meaningful operational accountability. However, PV operates entirely within the LR (load reduction) domain and has no interaction with Q (quality) factors, limiting cross-category integration compared to systems like DGNB or LBC.

## 8. DGNB NC 2020 International (Germany) [VERIFIED]

DGNB treats PV through its Life Cycle Assessment credit ENV1.1, where PV is assessed as a building component with a full lifecycle profile. The scheme explicitly references EN 15316-4-3 (thermal solar and photovoltaic systems) as a calculation standard, and the LCA methodology includes facade components — making facade BIPV assessable within the same framework as roof PV. The Agenda 2030 bonus for net-positive energy generation (where buildings generate energy for other users, recorded in the LCA) creates a meaningful incentive for maximum-area PV deployment including facade integration.

DGNB achieves the broadest cross-category integration in the dataset: PV contributes to ENV1.1 (LCA), TEC technical quality (envelope assessment), and ECO lifecycle cost — a genuine multi-dimension recognition. The system aligns with EU EPBD nearly-zero-energy requirements. No explicit BIPV/BAPV distinction is made in the credit language, but the LCA methodology is building-component-agnostic and naturally encompasses BIPV.

## 9. Passive House Classic/Plus/Premium v10c 2023 (Germany) [VERIFIED]

Note: The Passive House data was extracted from file 6.03_building_criteria_en.pdf, which was initially mapped to Green Star but is in fact the Passive House Institute Building Criteria version 10c (January 2023).

Passive House uses a pass/fail criteria structure rather than a points system. The Classic tier has no renewable energy generation requirement. The Plus tier mandates ≥60 kWh/m²a of on-site RE generation (relative to building footprint), and the Premium tier mandates ≥120 kWh/m²a. PV generation is counted toward the Primary Energy Renewable (PER) balance, with data sheets and system specifications required as verification evidence. Section 3.2.9 notes that "areas covered with rear ventilated solar collectors or photovoltaic panels (including the areas required between the panels) are excluded from the cool colour criterion" — a passive acknowledgment of facade-mounted PV without creating a dedicated pathway.

BIPV is not mentioned by name in the criteria document. The system's envelope-first philosophy means PV is treated as a supplementary generation technology rather than an integrated design element. However, the Plus and Premium generation thresholds are quantitatively demanding relative to low-energy building areas, effectively incentivising maximum surface use including facades. PHPP energy balance software inherently links PV generation to envelope performance.

## 10. Living Building Challenge 4.0 (USA/ILFI) [VERIFIED]

LBC sets the most ambitious renewable energy requirement of any system reviewed: Imperative I-08 (Net Positive Carbon) mandates that all projects supply 105% of their energy needs through on-site renewable energy on a net annual basis, with no combustion allowed. This must follow a 70% baseline energy reduction (Imperative I-07). The 12-month actual performance data requirement before certification is granted — not predicted energy, not commissioning, but measured operational data — makes LBC the gold standard for performance verification.

Facade PV is implicitly required for most project types to achieve the 105% net-positive target: building footprints alone are rarely sufficient for full RE generation. The Beauty Imperative I-19 explicitly encourages BIPV for aesthetic integration, making LBC the only system that addresses the design quality of PV integration alongside its energy performance. The Materials Petal (Imperative I-12) assesses lifecycle impacts of building materials including PV components. This multi-petal integration — energy, materials, and aesthetics — represents the most holistic BIPV framework in the dataset, even though the word "BIPV" does not appear explicitly in the credit language.

## 11. MINERGIE 2023 (Switzerland) [VERIFIED]

Note: Only a 2-page summary document was available for review; the full MINERGIE certification scheme details were not accessible in the mapped file.

MINERGIE integrates PV through the Minergie Coefficient (MKZ), which measures energy balance across the full building lifecycle. The 2023 standard assumes full roof PV as a baseline for MKZ calculation — i.e., the coefficient is penalised if available roof area is not utilised for PV. The fossil-free requirement is mandatory. The 2017 version explicitly required ≥10 Wp/m² ERA (Energy Reference Area) with a cap of 30 kWp, demonstrating a prescriptive PV mandate. Operational monitoring is required for buildings ≥1000m² ERA, providing formal verification.

BIPV is not named specifically, and facade PV has no dedicated credit pathway, though its contribution to the MKZ calculation is accepted. The MINERGIE-A (net-zero energy) standard represents very high ambition, and the MKZ baseline assumption of full roof PV is a stronger PV integration mechanism than any voluntary credit in points-based systems. The MINERGIE-P standard creates interaction between envelope performance and energy balance, analogous to Passive House's PHPP.

## 12. Green Globes 2021 NC (USA/Canada) [VERIFIED]

Green Globes addresses renewable energy under Section 3.4.1 (On-Site Renewable Energy, 30/1000 pts). The credit structure offers 5 points for completing a feasibility study (3.4.1.1) and up to 25 points for on-site RE generation percentage, using a life cycle cost-effectiveness (LCCE) filter to ensure economic viability. The feasibility study is optional (earning points) rather than mandatory, and BIPV is not mentioned anywhere in the reviewed sections. Solar PV is not named specifically; the credit refers generically to "on-site renewable energy systems."

Green Globes uses a self-assessment approach with third-party verification at the assessor level, making it less rigorous than commissioning-based systems. The percentage-of-energy metric is more accessible than GHG-based approaches, and the LCCE filter is notable for acknowledging that PV must be economically viable to earn credit — a realistic constraint for facade BIPV. The total weight of 3% for renewable energy is among the lowest in the dataset.

## 13. HQE v4 BD 2022 (France) [PARTIAL]

Note: The file reviewed (Certivea, March 2023) is a EU Green Taxonomy alignment report, not the full HQE certification scheme manual. Key findings are therefore limited.

HQE aligns with the EU Green Taxonomy for climate change mitigation and adaptation, and Certivea's taxonomy mapping confirms that HQE certification requirements overlap with DNSH (Do No Significant Harm) criteria for energy performance. The document references RE2020 (France's current building energy regulation, which replaced RT2012) as the underlying technical standard governing energy and renewable energy in HQE-compliant buildings. RE2020 introduced a lifecycle carbon approach and progressive renewable energy targets. No BIPV-specific language was found in the taxonomy report. Full assessment of HQE's PV and BIPV treatment requires the complete HQE certification scheme documentation.

## 14. Estidama Pearl Villa v1.0 (UAE) [VERIFIED]

Note: The file reviewed (Pearl Villa Rating System) is the residential version of Estidama. The commercial/community Pearl Building Rating System likely differs.

The Pearl Villa Rating System addresses renewable energy under RE-R1 (mandatory feasibility assessment) and RE-1 through RE-3 (scored credits). RE-3 awards up to 7 points through a combination of feasibility study (1 pt), on-site RE percentage tiers (1-5 pts for 3-25% of energy), and off-site RE procurement (1 pt). Solar photovoltaics are named in the submission document requirements. The prescriptive method sets absolute generation thresholds (500/1000 kWh/yr), while the performance method uses percentage-of-energy targets — a dual-pathway approach suited to the UAE climate where solar irradiance is exceptionally high.

BIPV is not mentioned by name. Shading PV is referenced in the hot climate context, acknowledging the value of dual-function PV elements. Post-occupancy energy performance monitoring is required for Pearl-rated buildings. The mandatory RE-R1 feasibility assessment represents meaningful design-stage integration, though no mandatory implementation follows from it.

## 17. GRIHA v2019 (India) [VERIFIED]

GRIHA Criterion 8 (Renewable Energy Utilization) awards up to 5 points out of 100, with a mandatory minimum of 5% RE for daytime-occupancy buildings. All MNRE-recognised renewable energy systems are eligible. The notable finding is that BIPV appears in GRIHA's official abbreviations list (page 17): "BIPV = Building-Integrated Photovoltaics" — one of only two systems in the dataset (alongside BEAM Plus) to formally define BIPV. However, BIPV is not specifically referenced in Criterion 8's scoring criteria or in Criterion 28 (which names solar photovoltaic panels generically). The abbreviation recognition indicates awareness of BIPV as a distinct technology without creating a specific credit pathway for it.

Innovation Criterion 30 provides a net-positive energy pathway where buildings generating more energy than they consume can earn innovation points — an incentive that implicitly rewards maximum-surface PV including facade integration. The MNRE recognition framework is efficient for practitioners but creates no distinction between high-performance integrated PV and conventional roof-mount systems.

## 18. LOTUS v2.0 (Vietnam) [VERIFIED]

LOTUS Credit E-6 (Renewable Energy, 2/55 pts, ~3.6%) awards points for on-site renewable energy contribution: 1 point at 0.5% and 2 points at 1.5% of building energy. Photovoltaic (PV) and Solar Thermal are explicitly listed among valid RE forms. The ambition level is very low by international standards, consistent with LOTUS's positioning as an accessible framework for the Vietnamese construction market. No BIPV language appears anywhere in the reviewed sections, no solar feasibility study is required, and no application type distinction is made between roof, facade, or other PV configurations. Verification requirements are not specified for E-6 specifically.

## 19. TREES NC v2 2017 (Thailand) [VERIFIED]

TREES Credit EA2 (Renewable Energy, 2/85 pts, ~2.4%) awards points for on-site renewable energy: 1 point at 0.5% and 2 points at 1.5% of annual energy cost. The credit explicitly names "solar cell" (solar PV), wind power, and biofuel-based energy as qualifying technologies, and notes that systems "may be sold back to the electricity grid" — one of the few systems to explicitly acknowledge grid export. No BIPV language appears. No solar feasibility study is required. Application type distinctions (roof/facade/window) are not made.

TREES's GI Innovation section (5 points total across 5 innovation credits) could in principle reward novel BIPV approaches, but the innovation pathway is generic and not specifically directed at renewable energy or PV integration. The credit structure is essentially identical to LOTUS in ambition level, consistent with both systems' role as introductory frameworks for their respective markets.

## Cross-System Findings

**BIPV explicitly named:** BEAM Plus v2.0 (EU5a credit language, page 313) and GRIHA v2019 (abbreviations list, page 17) are the only systems to formally name BIPV. All other systems use generic "renewable energy" or "on-site PV" language.

**Mandatory solar feasibility study:** BREEAM Ene04 (Criterion 9), Green Mark Stage 1 & 2 Verification, Estidama RE-R1, and LBC design charrette are the strongest mandates. BEAM Plus EU5a awards 1 credit for a feasibility study (encouraged but optional). LEED IPc1 and Passive House have no mandatory study.

**Highest ambition level:** LBC (105% net-positive, mandatory), MINERGIE-A (net-zero), Green Mark ZE/PE (100%/115%), Passive House Premium (120 kWh/m²a), and BREEAM exemplary (net-zero carbon) lead the dataset.

**Operational verification:** LBC (12-month measured data mandatory), Green Mark (12-month audit mandatory), MINERGIE (monitoring from 1000m² ERA), and ASGB (operational assessment) are the strongest. WELL, EDGE, LOTUS, TREES, and CASBEE-BD have no PV-specific operational verification.

**Cross-category integration:** DGNB (ENV1.1 + TEC + ECO), LBC (Energy + Materials + Beauty imperatives), and Passive House (PHPP integrates PV with envelope) achieve genuine multi-domain PV recognition. All other systems treat PV exclusively within their Energy category.
