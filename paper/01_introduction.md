# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 The Imperative of Building Decarbonisation

Buildings account for approximately 36% of global final energy consumption and nearly 40% of energy-related carbon dioxide emissions [IEA, 2023]. As urbanisation accelerates and floor area worldwide is projected to double by 2060, the building sector represents both the largest single source of avoidable emissions and one of the most cost-effective arenas for climate action [IPCC, 2022]. Achieving the targets set under the Paris Agreement requires not merely incremental improvements in building efficiency but a wholesale transformation of how buildings generate, manage, and account for energy. In this context, the relationship between the built environment and on-site renewable energy generation has moved from an optional enhancement to a structural necessity.

Solar photovoltaic (PV) technology has matured rapidly over the past two decades, with module costs declining by more than 90% since 2010 and global installed capacity surpassing 1.2 TW [IRENA, 2024]. The predominant deployment model—ground-mounted utility-scale plants or rack-mounted rooftop arrays—treats PV as an energy infrastructure system appended to buildings rather than integrated within them. While this approach is effective for energy generation, it leaves unrealised a substantial design opportunity: the building envelope itself, comprising roof planes, façades, windows, spandrel panels, shading louvres, and canopies, constitutes an enormous surface area that can serve simultaneously as weather barrier, thermal regulator, daylighting filter, architectural element, and electricity generator.

## 1.2 Building-Integrated Photovoltaics as a Multifunctional Building Component

Building-Integrated Photovoltaics (BIPV) is the technology class that realises this dual function. Unlike Building-Applied Photovoltaics (BAPV), in which conventional modules are mounted over an existing envelope, BIPV products replace a building element: a BIPV roof tile replaces a ceramic tile and generates electricity; a BIPV curtain-wall module replaces a spandrel glass panel; a semi-transparent BIPV glazing unit simultaneously transmits daylight, controls solar heat gain, and produces power. The International Electrotechnical Commission formalises this distinction in IEC 63092, which requires that a BIPV product perform the primary function of the building element it substitutes—structural support, weather protection, thermal insulation, or visual transparency—in addition to electricity generation [IEC, 2020].

This multifunctionality makes BIPV qualitatively different from conventional PV. A BIPV façade module influences a building's thermal performance (U-value, solar heat gain coefficient), its daylighting quality (visible light transmittance, glare), its material composition (embodied carbon, recyclability), its architectural expression, and its energy balance—all simultaneously. Yet this very complexity means that BIPV does not map cleanly onto any single credit or prerequisite within existing green building rating systems. A rooftop BIPV installation may be captured under a renewable energy credit; the same product's contribution to envelope thermal performance, to daylighting quality, to lifecycle carbon, or to design integration may go entirely unrecognised by the certification framework under which the building is assessed.

Despite growing scholarly and industry attention to BIPV as an energy technology, the question of how this multifunctional component is treated within the policy and certification instruments that govern green building practice has received limited systematic scrutiny. This gap is the motivating problem of the present study.

## 1.3 Green Building Rating Systems as Policy Instruments

Green Building Rating Systems (GBRS) occupy a pivotal position in the construction industry's transition toward sustainability. By attaching measurable performance credits to third-party-verified building attributes, GBRS translate abstract sustainability goals into concrete design requirements, procurement decisions, and operational benchmarks. Systems such as LEED (USA), BREEAM (UK), Green Star (Australia), Green Mark (Singapore), and DGNB (Germany) collectively certify hundreds of thousands of buildings globally and influence the design decisions of vastly more projects that pursue certification informally or use rating criteria as internal benchmarks [WorldGBC, 2023]. In many jurisdictions, GBRS compliance is now mandated for public buildings, embedded in green finance eligibility criteria, or required by institutional investors under environmental, social, and governance (ESG) frameworks.

The renewable energy provisions within most GBRS were developed during an era in which PV was treated primarily as an energy generation technology: a system added to a building to reduce its grid electricity consumption and the associated operational carbon. Credits and prerequisites were therefore written around metrics such as percentage of energy demand met by on-site renewables, reductions in Energy Use Intensity (EUI), or minimum renewable capacity thresholds. These framings largely persist today, even as BIPV has matured into a product category with dedicated international standards, established manufacturers, and a growing body of performance data. The consequence is that GBRS may systematically under-recognise—or structurally fail to recognise—the multi-dimensional performance contribution of BIPV.

Understanding how GBRS treat BIPV is not merely an academic exercise. If certification frameworks reward only the energy-generation dimension of BIPV while ignoring its contributions to envelope performance, daylighting, materials sustainability, and carbon accounting, designers and developers face a distorted incentive structure. A BIPV façade system that simultaneously meets thermal performance requirements, reduces embodied carbon relative to conventional cladding, contributes to daylight autonomy, and generates electricity may receive only a fraction of the certification credit that its total performance contribution warrants. Conversely, if GBRS innovation pathways or pilot credits are available but poorly documented, design teams may fail to pursue them. In either case, the certification framework fails to fulfil its potential as a driver of advanced building integration.

## 1.4 The Research Gap

A substantial body of literature has examined BIPV from technical perspectives—module efficiency, system integration challenges, thermal coupling effects, and lifecycle cost—and from market and policy perspectives—adoption barriers, feed-in tariff design, and industry development. A growing number of studies have assessed GBRS provisions for renewable energy broadly, and several comparative analyses of specific GBRS categories (energy, materials, indoor environment) have been published. However, a systematic, cross-system mapping of how BIPV's full range of performance dimensions is addressed within global GBRS has not previously been conducted.

The bibliometric analysis conducted as part of this study identified 2,330 papers in the intersection of BIPV and green building research published between 2000 and 2024. Of these, only 13 papers (0.56%) addressed the specific intersection of BIPV technology and green building certification systems. Among all BIPV-related papers, 87.3% named no specific GBRS in their analysis. This quantitative evidence confirms that the mapping of BIPV across rating systems represents a genuine and largely unexplored research gap.

## 1.5 Contribution of This Study

This paper presents the first comprehensive, multi-dimensional mapping of BIPV provisions across 21 major Green Building Rating Systems operating in 19 countries. The mapping framework encompasses 12 performance dimensions that span BIPV's full range of building functions: direct renewable energy generation across five application typologies (roof, façade, window/curtain wall, shading device, and glass-integrated PV); and seven indirect performance dimensions (thermal envelope, daylighting and visual comfort, materials and lifecycle assessment, carbon accounting, EV and storage synergy, design integration, and innovation pathways). Each dimension is scored on a four-level rubric (0 = not addressed; 1 = indirect or implied coverage; 2 = explicit credit recognition; 3 = dedicated BIPV-specific criteria), yielding a 21 × 12 matrix of 252 assessed cells.

The study proceeds in three phases. Phase 1 is a systematic bibliometric analysis of the BIPV-GBRS literature. Phase 2 is a structured manual content analysis of 31 GBRS technical reference documents, with credit-level citation of provisions relevant to each of the 12 dimensions. Phase 3 is the synthesis mapping, which aggregates credit-level findings into dimension scores, computes cross-system rankings, and identifies structural gaps in current GBRS coverage of BIPV.

The findings have direct implications for three audiences. For GBRS administrators and standards developers, the mapping identifies which dimensions of BIPV performance are systematically absent from certification frameworks, providing an evidence base for targeted credit development. For building designers and project teams, it provides a navigable guide to which GBRS provisions—including indirect credits not typically associated with PV—can be leveraged for BIPV installations. For researchers and policymakers, it establishes a rigorous baseline against which the evolution of GBRS provisions can be tracked as the BIPV market and supporting standards continue to develop.

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 describes the methodology of all three phases. Section 3 presents the bibliometric results. Section 4 presents the full mapping results across all 21 systems and 12 dimensions. Section 5 discusses the implications of the mapping patterns, including the D-DAY zero-coverage finding and the strong regional divergence in BIPV recognition. Section 6 concludes with recommendations for GBRS development and directions for future research.
