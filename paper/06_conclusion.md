# 6. Conclusion

This study has presented the first systematic, multi-dimensional mapping of Building-Integrated Photovoltaics provisions across 21 major Green Building Rating Systems operating in 19 countries. The 12-dimension framework, spanning five direct BIPV application typologies and seven indirect performance dimensions, generated a 252-cell assessment matrix scored against a four-level rubric based on direct content analysis of 31 official certification documents.

Four principal findings emerge from the analysis. First, GBRS coverage of BIPV is both limited and deeply uneven. The mean score across all 252 cells was 1.15/3.00, indicating that typical coverage is at the level of indirect or implied mention rather than explicit recognition. System-level total scores ranged from 4 (WELL) to 30 (LBC), a nine-fold range that confirms the GBRS landscape is highly stratified in its treatment of BIPV. Only five systems — LBC, DGNB, Green Mark, BREEAM, and MINERGIE — achieve multi-dimensional BIPV recognition across both energy and indirect performance domains.

Second, BIPV application typologies beyond rooftop PV are largely absent from certification frameworks. Window/curtain wall PV (mean 0.52) and glass-integrated PV (mean 0.43) recorded the two lowest dimension scores in the entire matrix, and no system provides dedicated criteria for shading device PV, window PV, or glass-integrated products. Only one system — BEAM Plus EU5a — uses the term "BIPV" anywhere in its credit language, and only one additional system — GRIHA — formally defines BIPV in its documentation, without creating any differentiated credit pathway. This BIPV naming gap is the surface manifestation of a deeper structural absence: certification frameworks treat all on-site PV generation equivalently, providing no incentive for the higher-cost, higher-performance building-integrated solutions that are architecturally necessary for high-density and high-rise contexts.

Third, the D-DAY zero-coverage finding reveals that daylighting certification provisions are completely decoupled from BIPV products that directly affect daylighting outcomes. Despite all 21 systems including substantive visual comfort and daylight quality credits, the extended keyword extraction found zero explicit links between daylighting credits and BIPV glazing or PV shading devices in any of the 17 scanned documents. This absence perpetuates an incentive structure in which BIPV façade solutions must comply with daylighting standards designed for conventional glazing while receiving no credit recognition for the solar control function that is frequently their primary architectural justification.

Fourth, indirect performance dimensions — particularly thermal envelope (mean 2.05), carbon accounting (mean 1.71), and daylighting (mean 1.81 in coverage, zero in direct linkage) — are broadly addressed in GBRS frameworks but are not connected to BIPV products as eligible compliance pathways. The mismatch between high indirect dimension scores and the absence of explicit BIPV guidance within those credits quantifies the performance recognition gap that BIPV project teams face.

These findings have direct implications for three practitioner communities. GBRS administrators and standards developers are presented with a specific, evidence-based account of which credit categories in which systems are most urgently in need of BIPV-specific guidance. Building designers and project teams have, for the first time, a navigable map of which GBRS provisions — including indirect credits not typically associated with PV — can potentially be leveraged for BIPV installations, and which systems offer the most favourable certification environment for BIPV-ambitious projects. Policymakers and sustainability finance professionals can use the cross-system ranking and dimension profiles to assess the adequacy of certification requirements in their jurisdictions and to identify the most targeted interventions for improving GBRS-driven incentives for BIPV adoption.

Future research should extend this work in three directions. First, the framework should be applied on a rolling basis as GBRS publish new version releases, tracking whether the credit developments recommended in Section 5 are incorporated and measuring the rate of convergence toward BIPV-explicit provisions. Second, the mapping should be extended to building codes and energy performance regulations — the mandatory layer of building governance that operates below voluntary certification frameworks — to assess whether the BIPV recognition gap at the GBRS level is mirrored or compensated at the regulatory level. Third, post-occupancy performance data from BIPV installations in certified buildings should be systematically collected and analysed to provide the empirical evidence base for differentiated BIPV credit valuation, addressing the verification gap identified in the discussion.

Building-integrated photovoltaics represents one of the clearest paths toward net-zero and net-positive energy performance in high-density urban contexts where roof area alone is insufficient for the generation targets that ambitious certification requires. The certification frameworks that govern green building practice have not yet adjusted to recognise this reality. This study documents that gap with precision and provides the analytical foundation for the credit-level changes needed to correct it.
