# 3. Results — Bibliometric Analysis

## 3.1 Corpus Overview and Temporal Trends

The merged and deduplicated Scopus corpus comprised 2,330 unique records spanning the period 2000–2024. The distribution of publications over time reveals a pronounced acceleration in BIPV-related research output from approximately 2015 onward, coinciding with the dramatic decline in PV module costs and the progressive tightening of building energy codes globally. Annual publication counts remained below 50 throughout the 2000s, rose to approximately 80–100 per year in the early 2010s, and exceeded 200 annually from 2018 onward, with the highest volumes recorded in 2022–2023 (Fig. topic_area_year). This trajectory broadly mirrors the growth curve of global PV installed capacity and confirms that BIPV-related scholarship has emerged as an active field rather than a niche subfield.

The four search queries contributed records with partial overlap: Query A (BIPV and building-integrated solar terms) yielded the most specialised results, while Query C (green building combined with photovoltaics) and Query D (named GBRS systems with solar) captured the most policy-oriented and comparative papers. After deduplication, the merged corpus represents the broadest available synthesis of scholarship at the intersection of photovoltaics and building sustainability.

## 3.2 Geographic Distribution

China was the most productive country in the corpus by first-author affiliation, contributing 57 papers, followed by South Korea (40 papers). The top ten countries—China, South Korea, the United Kingdom, the United States, Australia, Germany, Italy, Singapore, Spain, and the Netherlands—accounted for approximately 68% of all first-authored records. The concentration of output in East Asia and Western Europe is consistent with the geography of GBRS adoption and PV market activity, though it also reflects the well-documented publication biases of the Scopus database toward English-language journals.

Notably, countries that host major GBRS development programmes but have smaller English-language research outputs—such as Brazil (AQUA/HQE), Qatar (GSAS/QSAS), and India (GRIHA, IGBC)—are correspondingly underrepresented in the corpus despite the operational relevance of their rating systems to regional BIPV markets. This geographic skew reinforces the value of the direct GBRS document analysis (Phase 2), which is not subject to publication-language biases.

## 3.3 Topic Classification

The topic classification algorithm assigned each of the 2,330 papers to one of eight categories (Fig. topic_pie). The largest single category was Net-Zero / Zero-Energy Buildings (887 papers, 38.1%), reflecting the dominance of energy balance and operational performance as research lenses in the BIPV literature. BIPV Technical Performance was the second largest category (439 papers, 18.8%), followed by Policy / Regulation (254 papers, 10.9%) and BIPV Economics / LCA (133 papers, 5.7%).

The GBRS Comparative Analysis category—papers that explicitly compare two or more rating systems—contained 55 papers (2.4%), confirming that cross-system comparative work exists as a recognisable subfield but constitutes a small fraction of overall output. Urban Solar Potential papers accounted for another 13 papers (0.6%), largely consisting of GIS-based solar irradiation modelling studies that rarely engage with certification frameworks.

The most significant finding from the topic classification is the size of the BIPV+GBRS Cross category: only 13 papers (0.56%) were classified as explicitly bridging BIPV technology and green building certification criteria. These papers collectively represent the total prior systematic engagement with the question addressed by the present study. Examination of the 13 papers reveals that they are largely case-study accounts—typically reporting on a single certified building with a BIPV installation—rather than systematic comparative analyses of how certification frameworks treat BIPV. None presents a multi-system, multi-dimension mapping of the kind developed in this study.

## 3.4 GBRS Recognition in the Literature

The GBRS detection pass identified that 297 papers (12.7%) mentioned at least one named rating system. Of the remaining 2,033 papers (87.3%), no specific GBRS was referenced by name, confirming that the vast majority of BIPV scholarship is conducted without reference to any certification framework. This decoupling between the technical literature and the policy instrument literature is itself a finding of significance: BIPV researchers and practitioners operate largely outside the incentive structures that GBRS are designed to create.

Among papers that did name a GBRS, LEED was cited most frequently (116 papers, 5.0% of corpus), followed closely by Passive House (114 papers, 4.9%) and WELL (38 papers, 1.6%). The prominence of Passive House in the corpus is partly attributable to the substantial body of near-zero-energy-building research that references it as a performance standard even when the primary focus is PV integration rather than certification. BREEAM was mentioned in 19 papers (0.8%), and the full set of remaining 18 systems each appeared in fewer than 10 papers.

Regional GBRS were particularly underrepresented: DGNB appeared in only 2 papers (0.1%), HQE in 2 papers (0.1%), and Green Globes in 1 paper (0.04%). The absence of these systems from the corpus does not reflect their operational importance—DGNB is the dominant commercial green building framework in German-speaking Europe and certifies thousands of buildings—but rather the focus of the English-language research community on Anglo-American certification systems.

## 3.5 BIPV Application Type Distribution

Among the 2,330 papers, application type was classified as unspecified for 1,404 papers (60.3%), typically because the paper discussed PV in generic energy-modelling terms without specifying the installation typology. Generic PV or BAPV was identified in 508 papers (21.8%). Among papers that specified a BIPV typology, window and curtain-wall BIPV was the most common (171 papers, 7.3%), followed by façade BIPV (130 papers, 5.6%), rooftop PV (69 papers, 3.0%), and shading BIPV (48 papers, 2.1%).

The relatively high frequency of window and curtain-wall BIPV in the literature reflects the commercial maturity and architectural novelty of semi-transparent PV glazing products, which have attracted substantial research interest as high-visibility demonstration installations. Shading BIPV, by contrast, has generated proportionally fewer publications despite its architectural versatility, suggesting that this application typology is underserved by both research and certification frameworks.

## 3.6 Key Findings Extracted from the Corpus

The key-finding extraction identified several recurrent themes relevant to the GBRS mapping that follows. The most frequently cited barriers to BIPV adoption in the literature included higher upfront costs relative to conventional envelope systems, uncertainty about product standards and durability, limited installer expertise, and the absence of clear guidance from GBRS on how to credit BIPV's multifunctional performance [REF, REF, REF]. A widely cited finding from a Singapore-based stakeholder survey noted that "inclusion of BIPV in building codes or labels" was identified by practitioners as one of the most effective policy interventions for accelerating BIPV adoption [REF], directly motivating the present analysis of GBRS coverage.

The net-zero building literature consistently identifies roof-mounted PV as the most straightforward pathway to energy balance, while also documenting the structural difficulty of achieving net-zero energy in high-rise residential buildings where roof area per floor is insufficient—a context in which façade and curtain-wall BIPV become essential rather than optional [REF]. One study directly evaluated BIPV thermal transmittance "to attain the required green mark certification benchmarks in Singapore" [REF, 2021], representing a rare example of explicit BIPV-GBRS performance alignment in the empirical literature.

The absence of lifecycle assessment and embodied carbon considerations from most BIPV technical papers is also notable: the BIPV Economics/LCA category (133 papers, 5.7%) addresses whole-life costs and environmental impacts, but very few of these papers engage with the specific LCA credits within GBRS frameworks that could recognise PV modules as both a material choice and a carbon-reducing technology. This disconnect is reflected in the mapping results for Dimension H (Materials/LCA), discussed in Section 4.

Collectively, the bibliometric analysis confirms three structural features of the BIPV-GBRS research landscape: a very small body of direct intersection work, a strong Anglo-American GBRS bias in the English-language literature, and a persistent separation between the technical performance literature and the certification policy literature. The 12-dimension mapping presented in Section 4 is designed to address all three of these features by providing a comprehensive, evidence-based account of how GBRS of all geographic origins treat BIPV across its full range of building-relevant performance dimensions.
