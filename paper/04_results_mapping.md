# 4. Results — BIPV Mapping Across Green Building Rating Systems

## 4.1 Overview of the 21 × 12 Mapping Matrix

The mapping analysis assessed 252 cells across 21 Green Building Rating Systems and 12 performance dimensions. The complete matrix is visualised in Fig. mapping_master_heatmap. Aggregate statistics reveal a highly uneven distribution of scores: of the 252 cells, 91 (36.1%) received a score of 0 (not addressed), 63 (25.0%) received a score of 1 (indirect or implied coverage), 66 (26.2%) received a score of 2 (explicit recognition), and 32 (12.7%) received a score of 3 (dedicated criteria). The mean score across all cells was 1.15 out of 3, indicating that the typical GBRS dimension is addressed at the level of indirect or implied coverage rather than explicit recognition of BIPV as a distinct product category.

The mean scores differ substantially across the two dimension clusters. Cluster 2 (Indirect Performance Dimensions F–L) recorded a mean of 1.45 across all cells, compared to 1.00 for Cluster 1 (Direct BIPV Application Typologies A–E). This counterintuitive result — indirect performance dimensions scoring higher than direct application typologies — is explained by the architecture of GBRS credit structures: all 21 systems include thermal envelope credits (Dimension F) and nearly all include carbon accounting provisions (Dimension I), resulting in high scores for these dimensions even when BIPV is not explicitly named. By contrast, dedicated BIPV application typologies for façade (B), window (C), shading (D), and glass-integrated (E) products are largely absent from credit frameworks designed around energy generation rather than building integration.

The system-level total scores (maximum possible: 36) ranged from 4 (WELL) to 30 (LBC), with the full ranking presented in Table mapping_gbrs_totals. The nine-fold range between the lowest and highest-scoring systems confirms that the GBRS landscape is deeply stratified in its treatment of BIPV. The top five systems — LBC (30/36, 83%), DGNB (28/36, 78%), Green Mark (26/36, 72%), BREEAM (21/36, 58%), and MINERGIE (21/36, 58%) — all achieve genuine multi-dimensional BIPV recognition, while the bottom five — EDGE (6/36, 17%), WELL (4/36, 11%), CASBEE (9/36, 25%), LOTUS (9/36, 25%), and TREES (10/36, 28%) — address BIPV only through the most indirect or minimal provisions.

## 4.2 Dimension A: Roof PV (A_Roof_PV)

Dimension A recorded the second-highest mean score across all 12 dimensions (mean = 1.95), with 20 of 21 systems (95.2%) achieving at least an indirect mention and 18 of 21 (85.7%) achieving explicit recognition. Three systems (14.3%) scored 3 (dedicated criteria): LBC, Green Mark, and MINERGIE. WELL was the sole system to score 0, consistent with its exclusive focus on occupant health and its absence of energy generation credits.

The high coverage of Dimension A reflects the historical development of GBRS renewable energy credits around roof-mounted PV as the canonical installation type. All points-based systems that include renewable energy provisions — including LEED (EAc Renewable Energy), BREEAM (Ene01), Green Star (Energy category), DGNB (ENV1.1), BEAM Plus (EU5b), CASBEE (LR1.4.3), ASGB (Section 5.2.16), GRIHA (Criterion 8), IGBC, LOTUS (E-6), TREES (EA2), Green Globes (3.4.1), HQE, GSAS/QSAS, and Estidama (RE credits) — accept solar PV contributions to on-site energy generation, and rooftop systems are the implicitly assumed deployment model in all cases.

The three systems scoring 3 demonstrate qualitatively different levels of dedication. Green Mark GM:2021 achieves this through its mandatory Stage 1 and Stage 2 verification requirements, which require technical drawings and predicted energy generation calculations to be submitted for review, together with a post-occupancy 12-month energy audit. MINERGIE mandates full roof PV as a baseline assumption in its Minergie Coefficient calculation: the coefficient is penalised if available roof area remains unused, creating a binding obligation rather than an optional credit. LBC Imperative I-08 mandates 105% net-positive energy generation from on-site renewables, based on 12 months of measured operational data — the most demanding verification standard in the dataset and effectively a functional mandate for roof PV as a minimum contribution.

## 4.3 Dimension B: Façade PV (B_Facade_PV)

Dimension B exhibited a markedly lower mean score than Dimension A (mean = 1.29), despite applying to PV systems that involve only a change in installation plane. Nineteen of 21 systems (90.5%) scored at least 1, but only five systems (23.8%) achieved explicit recognition (score ≥ 2) and three (14.3%) achieved dedicated criteria (score = 3). The three systems scoring 3 were LBC, DGNB, and Green Mark.

The drop from Dimension A to Dimension B is structurally significant. In GBRS that credit renewable energy through percentage-of-energy or GHG-savings metrics — which includes LEED, BREEAM, BEAM Plus, CASBEE, Green Globes, GRIHA, IGBC, LOTUS, TREES, and most other points-based systems — the metric is application-type-agnostic: roof PV and façade PV contribute equivalently to the energy model, receiving no differential credit. The absence of a façade-specific pathway in these systems does not actively discourage façade BIPV, but it provides no positive incentive for choosing higher-cost integrated solutions over conventional roof installations.

The three systems achieving dedicated status demonstrate how façade BIPV can receive explicit credit within different architectural frameworks. DGNB ENV1.1 treats PV modules — whether roof or façade — as building components within its lifecycle assessment methodology, and the Agenda 2030 bonus for net-positive energy generation incentivises maximum-area deployment. LBC's net-positive energy mandate (Imperative I-08) combined with the Beauty Imperative I-19 — which explicitly encourages BIPV for aesthetic and architectural integration — constitutes the only instance in the dataset where design quality of PV integration is a formal criterion. Green Mark's Super Low Energy programme and Positive Energy (PE) tier requirement create a de facto façade BIPV incentive: achieving 115% of energy demand from on-site renewables using only the building footprint area is impractical for most building types, making façade and other non-roof surfaces essential.

## 4.4 Dimension C: Window/Curtain Wall PV (C_Window_Curtain_PV)

Dimension C recorded the second-lowest mean score in the entire matrix (mean = 0.52), with only 9 of 21 systems (42.9%) achieving any mention. No system scored 3, only two systems (DGNB and LBC, 9.5%) scored 2, and seven systems scored 1. Twelve systems (57.1%) scored 0 — including BREEAM, LEED, Green Star, BEAM Plus, CASBEE, Estidama, IGBC, Green Globes, GRIHA, LOTUS, TREES, and GSAS/QSAS.

This near-universal absence is particularly noteworthy given that window-integrated and curtain-wall BIPV is the most architecturally visible and commercially active non-roof BIPV category (representing 7.3% of papers in the bibliometric corpus). Semi-transparent BIPV glazing units — products that simultaneously function as structural glazing, solar control elements, and electricity generators — have no credit pathway in any of the 12 systems that scored 0. Their contribution to energy generation cannot be distinguished from rooftop PV in energy models (where they should be credited identically), and their contribution to envelope thermal performance, daylighting quality, and solar control is entirely uncredited in systems lacking cross-category provisions.

DGNB's score of 2 for this dimension reflects the system's building-component-agnostic LCA methodology: a curtain-wall BIPV module is assessable under ENV1.1 as a building component with an environmental product declaration, regardless of its location on the building envelope. LBC achieves score 2 through the combination of its net-positive energy mandate and the Beauty Imperative's recognition of architectural integration, which is sufficiently broad to encompass curtain-wall BIPV.

## 4.5 Dimension D: Shading Device PV (D_Shading_PV)

Dimension D recorded a mean score of 0.81, with 13 of 21 systems (61.9%) achieving some mention but no system achieving dedicated criteria (score = 3). Four systems (19.0%) achieved explicit recognition (score = 2), including LBC, DGNB, BREEAM, and Estidama Pearl. The latter is notable: Estidama's hot climate context makes shading devices architecturally integral to building design, and the Pearl Villa Rating System acknowledges PV shading in the context of the RE-1 to RE-3 credit structure.

PV-integrated external shading devices — louvres, brise-soleil, canopy structures, and fin systems — occupy an ambiguous position in GBRS frameworks. They function simultaneously as solar control elements (reducing cooling loads), as architectural elements (improving occupant comfort and visual quality), and as electricity generators. This triple function should, in principle, earn credit across at least three separate credit categories: energy generation, solar control, and possibly daylighting. In practice, the mapping reveals that no system provides guidance on how to apportion credit across these functions, and most systems treat shading PV only as a variant of on-site energy generation with no recognition of its solar control or architectural performance.

The eight systems that scored 0 for Dimension D — including LEED, Green Star, Green Mark, ASGB, BEAM Plus, CASBEE, GRIHA, IGBC, LOTUS, TREES, GSAS/QSAS, HQE, and Green Globes — represent a comprehensive failure of certification frameworks to recognise what is, in many climate zones, the most architecturally appropriate and thermally effective BIPV application type.

## 4.6 Dimension E: Glass-Integrated and Electrochromic PV (E_Glass_Energy_PV)

Dimension E recorded the lowest mean score in the entire matrix (mean = 0.43), with only 7 of 21 systems (33.3%) achieving any mention and no system achieving a score above 2. The two systems scoring 2 were DGNB and LBC. Fourteen systems (66.7%) scored 0, representing a complete absence of any provision relevant to glass-integrated or electrochromic PV products.

This near-total absence reflects the relatively recent commercial maturity of advanced BIPV glazing technologies — electrochromic BIPV, perovskite-based transparent PV, and luminescent solar concentrators — compared to the last major revision cycles of most GBRS documents. The finding suggests that certification frameworks have not yet responded to the emergence of dynamic glass products that can simultaneously modulate transmission, control solar heat gain, and generate electricity.

The very limited explicit recognition in DGNB and LBC is attributable to the same structural features identified in Dimensions C and D: DGNB's component-agnostic LCA methodology and LBC's net-positive mandate, both of which are sufficiently technology-neutral to accommodate emerging product categories without requiring specific credit revision.

## 4.7 Dimension F: Thermal Envelope (F_Thermal_Envelope)

Dimension F recorded the highest mean score of all 12 dimensions (mean = 2.05), with 20 of 21 systems (95.2%) achieving at least some mention, 19 (90.5%) achieving explicit recognition, and 4 (19.0%) achieving dedicated criteria. Only EDGE scored 0. This high coverage reflects the centrality of thermal envelope performance in virtually all GBRS frameworks, which consistently include credits or prerequisites for U-values, solar heat gain coefficients (SHGC), or equivalent overall envelope thermal performance metrics.

The significance of this finding for BIPV lies in what it implies about an uncaptured credit opportunity. BIPV façade products and semi-transparent BIPV glazing units are simultaneously envelope components and energy generators. A BIPV cladding panel that replaces a conventional spandrel and achieves an equivalent or better U-value should in principle earn credit under both the envelope performance credit and the energy generation credit. Yet the manual content analysis found that not one of the 21 systems provides explicit calculation guidance for how BIPV products should be assessed within thermal envelope credits — whether to treat opaque BIPV panels as equivalent to the wall assembly they replace, how to characterise the SHGC of BIPV glazing units, or how to handle the thermal coupling between PV cells and the building envelope below.

The extended extraction (Phase 2, second pass) confirmed this gap directly. Among the six envelope-relevant keywords applied to all scanned page ranges, the closest relevant material was found in GRIHA's SHGC compliance tables for fenestration (pages 82–84), which set orientation-specific maximum SHGC thresholds that semi-transparent BIPV glazing must satisfy — but without providing BIPV-specific guidance or acknowledging that BIPV might qualify differently from conventional glazing. Green Mark's Envelope Thermal Transfer Value (ETTV) and Residential Envelope Thermal Transfer Value (RETV) frameworks similarly create quantitative envelope assessment pathways that BIPV products could participate in, but the manuals are silent on the treatment of BIPV cladding within the ETTV formula.

## 4.8 Dimension G: Daylighting and Visual Comfort (G_Daylighting_Visual)

Dimension G produced one of the most striking findings in the entire mapping: while all 21 systems (100%) scored at least 1 and the mean score was 1.81, the extended extraction found zero direct keyword co-occurrences between daylighting/visual comfort credits and PV or BIPV references across all 17 scanned documents. This constitutes the D-DAY zero-coverage finding — the complete decoupling of daylighting certification provisions from BIPV products that inherently affect daylighting performance.

The universal score of at least 1 reflects the fact that all reviewed GBRS include substantive daylight quality provisions — spatial daylight autonomy (SDA), daylight factor (DF), annual sunlight exposure (ASE), or illuminance thresholds. The score reflects that BIPV products that affect glazing visible light transmittance (VLT) could in principle be assessed within these frameworks. The single system achieving a score of 3, DGNB, does so through its comprehensive indoor environment quality assessment that addresses visual comfort, glare, and daylight access in the same integrated framework as energy and materials performance.

The zero co-occurrence finding from the extended extraction, however, reveals that no system has updated its daylighting credit language to acknowledge that BIPV glazing and PV-integrated shading devices are directly relevant to daylighting outcomes. This gap has concrete consequences. A project team installing semi-transparent BIPV glazing on an office building façade must simultaneously demonstrate that the glazing meets daylighting requirements (SDA, DF, views) and energy generation targets. No GBRS provides a calculation pathway for the combined assessment, and no system explicitly permits PV shading devices to earn credit as solar control elements that improve glare metrics while generating power. The result is that BIPV façade solutions must comply with daylighting credits designed entirely around conventional glazing, while receiving no credit recognition for the solar control function that is frequently one of their primary architectural justifications.

BREEAM Hea01 (Visual Comfort) and LEED EQc7 (Daylight) both set performance targets that semi-transparent BIPV glazing can demonstrably meet at appropriate VLT values — yet neither system references BIPV in its credit guidance. This represents a directly addressable gap in both frameworks.

## 4.9 Dimension H: Materials and Lifecycle Assessment (H_Materials_LCA)

Dimension H recorded a mean score of 1.43, with 19 of 21 systems (90.5%) achieving at least an indirect mention and 8 (38.1%) achieving explicit recognition. Three systems (14.3%) achieved dedicated criteria: DGNB, LBC, and BREEAM. The eight systems scoring 0 — including LEED, ASGB, BEAM Plus, GSAS/QSAS, TREES, LOTUS, Estidama, and CASBEE — either lack LCA credits entirely or focus their materials credits on conventional building materials without addressing PV modules.

The variation in scores for Dimension H reveals a fundamental methodological difference between GBRS in how they conceptualise PV as a material. Systems that treat PV purely as an energy technology (LEED, BREEAM in its primary energy credits, most Asian systems) do not include PV modules within the scope of materials credits. LEED's Building Product Disclosure and Optimisation credits (MR) focus on construction materials and explicitly note that "equipment" is excluded; PV systems, classified as mechanical equipment in most building codes, therefore fall outside the scope of LEED's materials lifecycle assessment even though modern BIPV products are architecturally indistinguishable from conventional cladding.

DGNB ENV1.1 represents the contrary approach: PV is treated as a building component with a full lifecycle profile, and the LCA boundary explicitly includes PV generation systems using EN 15804-compliant EPDs and the EN 15316-4-3 calculation standard for solar systems. The extended extraction confirmed this as the only system where PV modules are explicitly included within the LCA credit boundary (pages 5, 15 of the ENV1.1 technical criteria document). DGNB's approach is directly aligned with IEC 63092's framing of BIPV as a building product with dual functions, and the system's component-level modularity means that lifecycle data for BIPV products can be incorporated without requiring credit revision.

LBC's Materials Petal (Imperative I-12) addresses embodied carbon and material composition of all building components including PV systems, and requires full material health transparency (Red List Free standard). BREEAM Mat01 (Lifecycle Impacts) includes whole-building LCA that can encompass PV installation, though specific BIPV guidance is absent.

## 4.10 Dimension I: Carbon Accounting (I_Carbon_Accounting)

Dimension I recorded a mean score of 1.71, with 20 of 21 systems (95.2%) achieving at least an indirect mention, 10 (47.6%) achieving explicit recognition, and 6 (28.6%) achieving dedicated criteria. The six systems scoring 3 — LBC, DGNB, Green Mark, BREEAM, MINERGIE, and Passive House — all include net-zero or net-positive energy/carbon pathways in which on-site PV generation plays a mathematically essential role.

The high overall coverage for Dimension I reflects the prominence of carbon and energy accounting frameworks within modern GBRS, which almost universally now reference operational carbon reductions as a primary performance metric. However, the scoring distribution for this dimension also reveals important methodological variation. Systems that use percentage-of-energy-demand metrics (LOTUS, TREES, GSAS/QSAS, Green Globes, IGBC) give PV generation credit that is proportional to its share of energy demand, with no distinction between the carbon intensity of the grid it displaces. Systems that use carbon-intensity-based metrics (LEED EAc, BREEAM Ene01 in its carbon-based Option 1) give variable credit based on the local grid emission factor, which can result in significantly different BIPV valuations across geographies.

The extended extraction found that carbon neutrality provisions in BREEAM, Green Star, and LBC mathematically require on-site renewable generation at scale — making BIPV a de facto necessity for top-tier certification even when not named. The finding from LBC page 27 is particularly clear: "Net zero energy" is defined as generating and exporting 105% of annual energy consumption through on-site production of renewable energy, and the 12-month operational measurement requirement means that this target must be demonstrably achieved, not merely modelled.

One additional nuance concerns the treatment of declining grid emission factors in carbon accounting. Several systems (including BREEAM and DGNB) use current-year grid emission factors, which means that as grids decarbonise over time, the carbon credit attributed to on-site PV generation declines. This structural feature creates a perverse dynamic: buildings that invest in BIPV today for long-term decarbonisation receive progressively less certification credit as the grid they were designed to partially replace becomes cleaner. No system reviewed addresses this temporal inconsistency explicitly.

## 4.11 Dimension J: EV and Energy Storage Synergy (J_EV_Storage)

Dimension J recorded a mean score of 0.76, with 13 of 21 systems (61.9%) achieving at least an indirect mention and three systems (14.3%) achieving explicit recognition. No system achieved dedicated criteria (score = 3) for this dimension. The three systems scoring 2 were LEED, Green Mark, and LBC.

The extended extraction confirmed the nature of the LEED EV credit (LT Credit: Electric Vehicles, page 39): the credit requires installation of Level 2 EVSE charging infrastructure for a defined percentage of parking spaces, but makes no reference to the energy source for charging. The credit incentivises EV infrastructure independently of on-site renewable generation, creating an opportunity rather than an obligation for the PV-EV integration pathway. Green Mark's Intelligence section (Annex 1) references smart energy management including EV integration, with PV generation as an implicit component of the smart grid stack.

The zero dedicated-criteria finding for Dimension J reflects the novelty of explicit PV-EV-storage integration in GBRS development. As of the most recent version of each system reviewed, none has created a credit specifically rewarding solar-charged EV infrastructure or co-located PV and battery storage as a package — despite the commercial maturity of PV+BESS+EVSE integration solutions. This gap is significant because it means that one of the most compelling BIPV value propositions for commercial buildings — the ability to generate solar electricity and distribute it immediately to an on-site vehicle fleet — is entirely unrecognised in certification frameworks.

## 4.12 Dimension K: Design Integration (K_Design_Integration)

Dimension K recorded a mean score of 1.10, with 12 of 21 systems (57.1%) achieving some mention, 7 (33.3%) achieving explicit recognition, and 4 (19.0%) achieving dedicated criteria. The four systems scoring 3 were LBC, Green Mark, BREEAM, and LEED — a grouping that reflects the strongest mandatory design-process requirements in the dataset.

BREEAM Ene04 Criterion 9 mandates a Low/Zero Carbon technology feasibility study at Concept Design stage, requiring written evidence that the applicability and cost-effectiveness of all LZC technologies including solar PV has been evaluated before design is frozen. This is the strongest mandatory design-integration requirement among all points-based systems reviewed, and it creates a structural obligation for project teams to consider PV (including BIPV) at the earliest design stage, when massing, orientation, and façade configuration decisions are still available as variables. Green Mark's Stage 1 Submission requirements similarly mandate solar feasibility assessment with expected generation calculations, making it the only system outside BREEAM in which on-site solar analysis is a verifiable mandatory deliverable.

LEED IPc1 (Integrative Process, 1 point) encourages solar analysis as part of early design discovery through its energy-related systems analysis requirement, but falls short of a mandate — the credit is optional and worth a single point. The extended extraction confirmed that LBC's Place Petal mandates a site-level analysis of solar access and PV potential as part of the charrette process, while Passive House's PHPP software inherently links envelope design to PV generation potential through the coupled energy balance model.

The seven systems scoring 0 for Dimension K — ASGB, GSAS/QSAS, CASBEE, LOTUS, TREES, WELL, and EDGE — include no design process requirements related to solar analysis or PV feasibility, meaning that for buildings certified under these systems, BIPV integration is entirely dependent on client or designer initiative rather than framework incentive.

## 4.13 Dimension L: Innovation Pathways (L_Innovation)

Dimension L recorded a mean score of 1.24, with 17 of 21 systems (81.0%) achieving at least an indirect mention and 7 (33.3%) achieving explicit recognition. Two systems (9.5%) achieved dedicated criteria: LBC and DGNB. The four systems scoring 0 — ASGB, GSAS/QSAS, LOTUS, and TREES — lack any innovation or exemplary performance pathway.

LBC's Innovation Petal structure and the Imperative I-19 Beauty requirement together constitute the most comprehensive innovation pathway in the dataset, rewarding both technical novelty and aesthetic integration of BIPV. DGNB's Transformation pathway and the Agenda 2030 bonus specifically reward net-positive energy performance that goes beyond the standard criteria, providing a defined credit structure for projects that use maximum-area BIPV to achieve net-positive generation.

LEED's Innovation credits (up to 5 points) provide a generic pathway that can be pursued for BIPV applications beyond the standard credit scope — for example, for façade or curtain-wall BIPV systems that demonstrate performance exceeding what rooftop systems alone could achieve. BREEAM's Excellent and Outstanding performance thresholds, and BEAM Plus's exemplary performance credits, create similar incentive structures. However, none of these generic innovation pathways includes specific guidance for BIPV, meaning that projects must make novel credit arguments on a case-by-case basis rather than following a defined pathway.

## 4.14 Cross-System Rankings and Cluster Analysis

The cross-system total score ranking (Fig. mapping_gbrs_profiles; Table mapping_gbrs_totals) reveals three distinct performance tiers within the 21-system dataset.

The **top tier** (score ≥ 20/36) comprises five systems: LBC (30), DGNB (28), Green Mark (26), BREEAM (21), and MINERGIE (21). These systems are characterised by at least three dimensions achieving dedicated criteria (score = 3), mandatory or strongly incentivised design-process requirements (Dimension K), and genuine multi-domain recognition of BIPV extending beyond the energy credits alone. All five have reached advanced maturity in their current version, have been recently revised, and operate in jurisdictions with active BIPV markets.

The **middle tier** (score 10–19) comprises ten systems: Green Star (19), Estidama Pearl (19), BEAM Plus (17), Passive House (15), HQE (15), Green Globes (13), ASGB (12), LEED (11), IGBC (11), GRIHA (11), and GSAS/QSAS (10). This grouping includes systems with strong energy ambition but limited cross-category integration (Passive House, LEED), systems with explicit BIPV recognition in narrow provisions (BEAM Plus EU5a for GRIHA's abbreviations list), and regional systems with limited documentation (HQE, GSAS/QSAS, IGBC). LEED's position in the middle tier despite its global prominence — scoring 11/36, equal to IGBC and GRIHA — reflects the narrow scope of its renewable energy credits relative to the breadth of the 12-dimension framework, and particularly its weak performance on Dimensions C (Window/Curtain Wall), D (Shading), E (Glass-Integrated), H (Materials/LCA), and J (EV/Storage).

The **lower tier** (score ≤ 10) comprises six systems: TREES (10), LOTUS (9), CASBEE (9), EDGE (6), and WELL (4). These systems share limited or absent renewable energy ambition (TREES, LOTUS at 1.5% thresholds), absence of cross-category integration, or fundamental scope misalignment (WELL's health focus, EDGE's accessibility focus). CASBEE's position in the lower tier reflects both its ratio-based BEE methodology, which makes point-equivalent comparisons difficult, and the genuine absence of PV-specific provisions across most of the framework's non-energy assessment dimensions.

Two findings are noteworthy in the geographic dimension of the ranking. All three systems from European countries appear in the top tier (LBC/ILFI, DGNB, BREEAM, MINERGIE), while four of the five lowest-scoring systems are from Southeast Asia or Japan (LOTUS/Vietnam, TREES/Thailand, CASBEE/Japan, EDGE/global but IFC-driven for developing markets). Green Mark (Singapore) is the notable exception, achieving third overall despite its tropical market context, reflecting Singapore's exceptionally ambitious national solar deployment targets and the mandatory verification structure of the rating system. The regional pattern is discussed further in Section 5.

## 4.15 Special Finding: BEAM Plus and the BIPV Naming Gap

One finding from the manual content analysis warrants specific emphasis: BEAM Plus v2.0 is the only GBRS in the dataset that explicitly names "BIPV" in its credit language. Credit EU5a (Solar Energy Feasibility Study) states: "Conduct a feasibility study to evaluate the potential of standalone and building-integrated installation in harnessing solar energy including photovoltaic and solar water heating. 1. Consideration of PV, BIPV or solar thermal potential installation." This sentence constitutes the clearest direct acknowledgment of BIPV as a distinct technology pathway in any certification framework reviewed.

The significance of this finding extends beyond BEAM Plus itself. The fact that 20 of 21 systems — including globally dominant frameworks such as LEED, BREEAM, and Green Star — do not use the term "BIPV" anywhere in their credit language means that project teams seeking to apply for credit with BIPV products must self-identify the applicable credit pathways, argue the equivalence of BIPV products to the assumed BAPV or non-integrated systems, and navigate a framework that was not designed with their application in mind. The absence of the word is symptomatic of a deeper structural absence: BIPV's multifunctional nature is not recognised, and its potential credit contribution across multiple dimensions is not claimed.

The complementary finding from GRIHA — that the system formally defines BIPV in its abbreviations list (page 17: "BIPV = Building-Integrated Photovoltaics") without creating any BIPV-specific credit pathway — illustrates an intermediate condition: systems that are aware of BIPV as a distinct technology category but have not yet translated that awareness into differentiated credit structures.
